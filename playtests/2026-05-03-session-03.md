# Session 3 — 2026-05-03

## Goal
Observe whether 5-player count changes the enchantment/Drought dynamics identified in Session 1, and whether the counter refresh rule remains confusing at higher player count.

## Hypothesis being tested
If the counter refresh rule is rewritten with a concrete turn-step example, new players will understand it without verbal explanation.

*(Note: this session did not directly test the rewritten rule — counter rule had not yet been revised. Session functioned as continued observational data on the enchantment/capacity problem.)*

## What happened
- Session ran with 5 players (Sam + 4 others)
- Game ended when the Source ran out — session stopped at that point due to outside circumstances
- Enchantment uptake extremely low across all 5 players
- Sam obtained 1 enchantment via a multi-turn workaround: unlearned a 4-card conjuration, re-learned it as a 3-card conjuration on the next turn, then learned the enchantment the turn after (using the card taken back from the original conjuration) — a 2-turn "overhead" cost just to create learning capacity
- Only 1 other player learned an enchantment, and only on the final round of the Source
- All players expressed feeling limited by low capacity, particularly for learning actions
- One player specifically noted the impossibility of focusing on enchantments given how many competing values were on the table from conjurations and transfigurations
- That same player suggested adding a fifth suit for 5+ players as a potential fix

## What worked
- Session reached end-of-Source, providing a complete data point at 5-player count
- The enchantment problem manifested clearly and consistently across multiple players, making it easy to observe

## What failed
- Enchantment uptake: only 2 out of 5 players learned any enchantment at all across the entire game
- Learning capacity felt universally constrained — this was not a single-player complaint but a shared experience
- Sam had to "burn" two extra turns just to create room for one enchantment, indicating the capacity system does not naturally support enchantment pursuit as a viable strategy

## What confused players
- The unlearn mechanic's scope: does unlearning require taking back **all** cards of a spell, or can any number of components be taken back as long as the spellbook remains valid without rearranging? This ambiguity surfaced during play and was not resolved by the current rules

## Triaged feedback

### Keep (act on)
- **Universal capacity constraint on learning actions** — All 5 players felt limited. This is a multi-player observation matching directly observed behaviour (only 2 enchantments across 5 players). Points to a core design problem: the capacity system does not support enchantment pursuit as a viable path.
- **Enchantment uptake is critically low at 5 players** — Only 2/5 players learned any enchantment. This replicates and amplifies the Session 1 finding. The problem is not a 2-player artefact; it is structural.
- **Unlearn scope ambiguity** — The question of whether partial unlearning is permitted (some cards vs all cards of a spell) surfaced mid-game across multiple players. The rule is either unclear or unspecified. Needs resolution.

### Clarify (probably communication, not design)
- **Enchantment timing / focus impossibility** — One player said it felt impossible to focus on learning an enchantment because of competing values from conjurations and transfigurations. This *may* be a clarity problem: if the player didn't have a mental model for how to build toward enchantments, clearer turn-planning guidance might help. Needs one more session to confirm before treating as design.

### Ignore for now
- **Fifth suit for 5+ players** — Proposed by one player as a solution to the enchantment scarcity problem. This is a solution proposal, not a problem report — and it would be a significant content addition. §14: do not act on single-player solution proposals; separate the problem (enchantment scarcity) from the fix. ## Diagnosis

**Observed:** At 5 players, only 2 enchantments were learned across the entire game. All players reported feeling capacity-constrained, especially for learning. Sam required a 2-turn overhead cost to create space for one enchantment. The game ended at Source depletion — session was cut short but the enchantment problem was fully visible before it ended.

**Expected:** Players should be able to pursue enchantments as a viable strategy without burning multiple turns of overhead. Learning capacity should feel like an interesting constraint, not a wall.

**Mismatch:** Enchantment pursuit is effectively a 3-turn investment (unlearn, restructure, learn) for a player who isn't pre-positioned — this is too costly relative to other paths. At 5 players the Source depletes faster, making the timing window for enchantments even narrower.

**Likely cause (design problem, not clarity):** The learning capacity system was balanced around 2-player pace or lower card counts. At 5 players, Source depletion is faster, and with more conjurations and transfigurations on the table consuming components, the remaining "room" for enchantments is structurally insufficient. This is not a rulebook explanation failure — Sam understood the rules perfectly and still needed 2 extra turns. The mechanic itself is not rewarding enchantment pursuit.

**Decision:** Test again — with a targeted change. Specifically: investigate whether a partial-unlearn rule (take back any subset of a spell's components, not all) would reduce the overhead cost of pivoting toward enchantments.

## Next hypothesis

If partial unlearning is permitted (any number of components from a spell can be taken back without unlearning the whole spell, as long as the spellbook remains valid), then players at 5-player count will be able to pursue enchantments without multi-turn overhead, and enchantment uptake will increase.
