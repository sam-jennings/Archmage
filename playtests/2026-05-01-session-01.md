# Session 1 — 2026-05-01

## Goal
First external playtest with a new player. Observe whether the game can be taught, played end-to-end, and generates enough engagement to want to return. Specifically: does the core loop make sense to a strategic player?

## Hypothesis being tested
_No hypothesis set at time of session (STATE.md: "None set")._

## What happened
- First time playing with printed board and reference cards (upgrade from Uno prototype)
- Sam taught the game; total time from explanation to final score calculation was 1.5 hours
- Player (strategic type, tends to deliberate) sometimes forgot which stage he was on or whether he'd drawn yet
- Player achieved a Perfect Transmutation of size 15 — score table on the webpage didn't cover values that high
- Both players each focused on two different suits over the course of the game
- Both players had 4-card enchantments at the Drought — this stalled Phase 2 into "take cards one at a time" and deferred spellbook organisation to the final turn
- Counter refresh rule caused confusion despite being on both the reference card and the board

## What worked
- Board was well-received ("board is good")
- Game phases flowed naturally — player noticed this unprompted
- Game ran successfully end-to-end with a new player
- Strategic player engaged with optimisation and blocking — the competitive tension worked at some level
- Perfect Transmutation of size 15 achieved, demonstrating the mechanic is reachable

## What failed
- Reference cards are hard to read — legibility/layout issue
- Counter refresh rule remained confusing even after repeated explanation and reading — persistent, not fixed by re-explaining
- No narrative or story experienced — player didn't notice any thematic arc
- Session length (1.5 hours for 2 players) is likely too long for the target experience
- Score table on webpage doesn't accommodate high scores (max PT size not covered)
- 4-card enchantments at Drought may be overpowered — effectively neutralised Phase 2 dynamics

## What confused players
- **Counter refresh on learning actions**: Player was confused even after Sam explained it and he read it on both reference card and board. Suggests the rule itself may be unintuitive, not just poorly described.
- **Enchantment effect timing**: Effects of playing or unlearning an enchantment need to be clarified as starting "next turn" — otherwise players can abuse the counter system. This gap wasn't caught during play but Sam identified it as a rule that needs explicit clarification.
- **Turn stage tracking**: Player (who thinks long on his turns) repeatedly forgot which stage he was in and whether he'd drawn a card yet.
- **Terminology**: "Transfiguration" and "Perfect Transmutation" found hard to say and remember.

## Best moment
Player achieving a Perfect Transmutation of size 15 — the mechanic is aspirational and reachable.

## Worst moment
Phase 2 stalling: both players had 4-card enchantments at the Drought, which meant the second phase became a slow card-by-card take rather than the intended spellbook organisation tension.

## Triaged feedback

### Keep (act on)
- **Counter refresh rule is persistently confusing** — Confusion survived explicit explanation + reading the rule on two surfaces. This is either a design problem (the rule is unintuitive) or a very serious clarity failure. Either way it needs resolving. Direct observed behaviour.
- **4-card enchantments at Drought are potentially overpowered** — Sam directly observed this: both players stalled Phase 2, deferred spellbook organisation to the last turn. Points to a structural balance/design issue worth testing.
- **Score table doesn't cover high Perfect Transmutation scores** — Bug/omission in the web app. PT of size 15 was achieved; table didn't go that high. Direct observed failure.
- **Session length 1.5 hours for 2 players** — Too long for the implied experience. This is a pacing/complexity signal worth tracking as player count scales.
- **No thematic narrative experienced** — Player didn't notice any story arc. This aligns with the target experience description ("wizard / spell-building fantasy") — the game should feel thematic. Only one player, but corroborated by other notes about themed cards.

### Clarify (probably communication, not design)
- **Reference cards hard to read** — Layout/legibility issue, not a mechanical one. Needs a redesign pass.
- **Turn stage tracking during long deliberation** — Player forgets which stage he's on. Likely a turn-tracker or clearer stage-summary UX fix, not a design change.
- **Enchantment effect timing ambiguity** — Sam identified the gap himself (effects start next turn). A rules-text clarification, not a mechanic change.
- **Terminology difficulty (Transfiguration, Perfect Transmutation)** — One player, and partly personal preference. Worth simplifying if possible but not a design emergency.

### Ignore for now
- **Steal-cards mechanism suggestion** — One player's proposed solution to wanting more conflict. The problem (not enough conflict with 2 players) is worth noting, but the solution is a scope addition. Anti-Drift Rule 1: don't add content to fix an experience gap without testing the core loop first.
- **"More fun with more players"** — Valid observation but not actionable at 2-player testing stage; this is expected and not a design fault.
- **Comparison to Chin Chan / wants social deduction** — Player's reference game is social deduction (rates it 10/10). Archmage Ascension is explicitly not targeting social deduction players. Outside target audience framing.
- **Themed card art suggestion** — Corroborates the thematic gap issue (logged under Keep) but the specific suggestion is a polish/content item deferred per Anti-Drift Rule 2.
- **2-player luck perception** — Player felt 2 players made it more luck-based. Single player at a count that amplifies variance. Worth monitoring but not acting on yet.

## Diagnosis

**Observed:** Counter refresh rule confused a strategic, literate player repeatedly despite verbal explanation and two written sources. Enchantment timing has an exploitable gap. 4-card enchantments at Drought neutralised the Phase 2 spellbook-organisation dynamic. No thematic narrative was felt. Reference cards are illegible.

**Expected:** Counter system understood after explanation; Phase 2 generates the intended tension of organising a growing spellbook under time pressure; game feels thematically coherent.

**Mismatch:** The counter rule is either too unintuitive to teach or is expressed in language that actively misleads. The enchantment timing gap is a rules-text omission that could be exploited. The Drought enchantment situation suggests a balance ceiling that collapses Phase 2 dynamics. Thematic texture is absent.

**Likely cause:** Counter refresh rule may conflict with player mental models (counters as resources, not capacity). Enchantment timing not stated explicitly enough in rules. 4-card enchantment limit at Drought may be too high for 2-player games, or the mechanic needs a cap. Thematic gap is partly a component/art issue (deferred) but partly that the mechanics don't signal a narrative arc.

**Decision:** Clarify enchantment timing in rules text. Investigate whether the counter refresh rule needs redesigning or just a rewrite. Test whether a lower enchantment cap or different Drought trigger would restore Phase 2 dynamics. Fix score table on webpage. Redesign reference cards for legibility.

## Next hypothesis
If the counter refresh rule is rewritten to use a concrete turn-step example, and enchantment timing is made explicit, then new players will understand both rules without verbal explanation.
