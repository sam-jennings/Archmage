# Archmage Ascension — Single Session Playtest Guide
## New Player (2-Player) | Critical Diagnostic Session

> **Your goal today is not to show off a good game. It is to find out what's broken.**
> 
> The best outcome of this session is a clear diagnosis of the next problem to solve — not validation that things are fine.

---

## Before You Start: Set Your Hypothesis

Before your player arrives, write down one sentence answering each of these:

**What is the thing I most need to learn from this session?**
→ e.g. *"Does a new player understand what they are supposed to be doing and why?"*

**What specific thing am I watching for?**
→ e.g. *"Does the Conjuration/Transfiguration distinction make intuitive sense, or do I have to explain it twice?"*

**What decision will this session help me make?**
→ e.g. *"Whether to keep the counter system as-is, or simplify it."*

Write these down before you play. Do not change them after. This stops you from unconsciously adjusting your reading of the session to suit whatever mood you're in afterwards.

---

## Framing the Session for Your Player

Say this (or something close) before you begin:

> *"I'm working on a card game and I need critical feedback — the kind that helps me fix it, not the kind that makes me feel good. The most useful thing you can do is tell me the moment something feels confusing, slow, or pointless. Don't protect my feelings. I specifically need to know what doesn't work."*

Then add:

> *"At a few points during the game I'm going to pause and ask you what you're thinking. That's normal — I'm watching the game, not just playing it."*

This primes them for honesty and makes mid-game check-ins feel expected rather than intrusive.

---

## Teaching the Game (Keep It Short)

**Your rule:** Explain only what they need to survive the first turn. Everything else, teach when it comes up.

### Step 1 — One-sentence pitch (do this first, before touching any components)

> *"You're a wizard building spells in your Spellbook. You collect magical components each turn, arrange them into spells, and cast those spells to get more components. The wizard with the most powerful Spellbook when the deck runs out wins."*

Pause. Ask: "Does that make sense as a concept?" Fix any fundamental confusion before you touch a card.

### Step 2 — Show the table layout

Point to each area and say its name and one-line purpose:

- **The Source** — the deck you draw from. When it runs out, the game enters its final phase.
- **The Array** — five face-up cards you can grab instead of drawing.
- **Your hand** — components you're working with.
- **Your Spellbook** — spells you've already built. This is what scores points.
- **Arcane Reserve** — discarded cards. They matter later.

Don't explain the Drought yet. It's too early and it'll confuse them.

### Step 3 — Explain the four spell types (use examples with real cards)

Lay out four physical examples from the deck as you explain each one:

1. **Conjuration** — "Three or more of the same energy type. When you cast it, you draw more cards."
2. **Transfiguration** — "Three or more cards in sequence, any energies. When you cast it, you swap cards with the Array."
3. **Enchantment** — "Three or four cards of the same value. It's always active — it gives you more capacity without being cast."
4. **Perfect Transmutation** — "Sequential AND same energy. Rare. Does both Conjuration and Transfiguration effects at once."

Keep the examples on the table so they can refer back.

### Step 4 — Explain the counter system in one go

> *"You start with 1 counter. Each turn: you use your counters to cast spells, then you pick them all back up (that's the Recall), then you use them again to Learn, Empower, Reshape, or Unlearn spells in your Spellbook. Counters aren't spent — they reset every turn. The only way to get more is by learning an Enchantment."*

Do a physical demo with your own hand: draw a card, cast one spell, recall your counter, do one learning action. Narrate as you go.

### Step 5 — Explain the Binding before you start

> *"Before we begin, we each put one card from our hand into the Arcane Reserve. This is just setup — it's not part of your strategy yet."*

### Step 6 — Don't explain scoring yet

Wait until the end of the game or until they ask. Early scoring explanations create anxiety and change how they play. You want natural first-timer behaviour, not optimised behaviour.

## During the Game: What to Watch

**Your job during play is 80% observation, 20% participation.**

Print or write this checklist. Fill it in as you play — not after.

### Confusion Signals (write down the exact moment and what triggered it)

- [ ] Player asks what a word/phase means more than once
- [ ] Player reaches for the wrong pile / does an action in the wrong order
- [ ] Player attempts something that isn't a legal move (note what they tried)
- [ ] Player looks stuck without knowing why they're stuck
- [ ] Player asks "wait, so what am I supposed to be doing right now?"
- [ ] Player misidentifies a spell type (e.g. thinks a Conjuration is a Transfiguration)
- [ ] Player forgets to Recall before their Learning Phase
- [ ] Player treats counters as a resource rather than a capacity (tries to "save" them)

### Engagement Signals

- [ ] Player checks their phone / loses focus (note when in the turn cycle)
- [ ] Player's eyes light up at a particular moment (note what triggered it)
- [ ] Player says "ooh" or reacts positively to the Array (good signal for that mechanic)
- [ ] Player becomes visibly bored or passive during a phase
- [ ] Player starts talking about what they're going to do next turn (invested)
- [ ] Player expresses frustration (note whether it's rules-frustration or game-frustration — these are different problems)

### Decision Quality Signals

- [ ] Player makes choices quickly with apparent confidence (the decision space is clear)
- [ ] Player stares at their hand for a long time without acting (decision paralysis — possibly too many options, possibly unclear goal)
- [ ] Player ignores the Array entirely (either they don't understand it or it's not useful)
- [ ] Player never builds a Transfiguration (maybe the mechanic is too complex for value it offers)
- [ ] Player ignores Enchantments (possibly underselling them, possibly they feel low-priority)
- [ ] Player asks "is this a good move?" (good sign — they care. Bad sign — they can't evaluate it themselves)

### Pacing Signals

- [ ] Turns feel fast and snappy (good)
- [ ] Turns drag out significantly (note where the delay is: Casting? Learning? Both?)
- [ ] The endgame (Drought) felt satisfying or anti-climactic (critical — note their reaction)
- [ ] Game ended at approximately the right time or overstayed its welcome

## Mid-Game Check-In (use this once, around mid-game)

Pause naturally — don't make it feel like a quiz. Ask only one or two:

1. *"What are you trying to do right now?"* — You're testing whether they have a strategy or are just reacting.
2. *"Is anything confusing?"* — Give them permission to say yes.
3. *"What's the most useful thing you've gotten from the Array so far?"* — Tests whether the Array feels meaningful.

Don't answer their questions with more rules explanations during this check-in. Just listen. Note what they say word-for-word if you can.

---

## End of Game: The Debrief

**Do the debrief immediately after the game ends.** Memory fades fast and so does the emotional state you want to capture.

Say: *"Before I explain the scoring or anything else — while it's fresh — I want to ask you a few questions. There are no right answers. The goal is to help me fix the game."*

### The Four Core Questions (from the course — ask all four, in this order)

1. **"What was your favourite part of the game?"**
   — Let them answer fully. Don't prompt. Note exactly what they say.

2. **"What was your least favourite part?"**
   — This is where the gold is. Don't defend anything. Don't explain. Just say "tell me more" if they're brief.

3. **"What confused you?"**
   — Probe for specifics: "When did that happen? What were you trying to do? What did you expect to happen instead?"

4. **"Was it fun? Why or why not?"**
   — Listen for *what kind* of fun, or *what kind* of unfun. "It felt thinky" is different from "I felt lost."

### Follow-up questions (only use the ones that feel natural)

- *"If you played again right now, what would you do differently?"* — If they have an answer, the game taught them something. If they shrug, it didn't.
- *"Was there anything you wanted to do that you couldn't?"* — Tests for missing options or blocked strategies.
- *"What did the scoring feel like when we tallied up?"* — Did it feel earned or arbitrary?
- *"Is there a moment you'd want to tell a friend about?"* — The aspirational moment test. These are the moments worth building around.
- *"Did the theme make sense? Did the Spellbook feel like a Spellbook?"* — Tests theme integration.
- *"Was there a point where you felt like you couldn't catch up / were already winning?"* — Balance signal.

### The one question you probably won't want to ask (but should)

*"If you were designing this game, what's the one thing you'd change?"*

People give solution-shaped answers ("I'd add X") that aren't always right — but the *problem* underneath the suggestion almost always is. Listen past the solution to the frustration it's coming from.

## After They Leave: Your Diagnosis

Do this within one hour of the session ending, while your memory is fresh.

### Step 1 — Write your raw observations

Just bullet points. Everything you noticed. Don't filter yet.

### Step 2 — Sort by category

Separate your observations into:
- **Clarity problems** — they didn't understand how something works
- **Design problems** — they understood it but it felt wrong, slow, or pointless
- **Pacing problems** — the game's rhythm was off somewhere
- **Communication problems** — the rules or card text failed to convey the intent

> **The key distinction:** If re-explaining it differently would have fixed it → clarity problem. If better explanation still wouldn't fix it → design problem. Only fix design after you've ruled out clarity.

### Step 3 — Apply the Diagnosis Format

For the single most important thing you noticed, write:

| Field | Your answer |
|-------|-------------|
| **Observed** | What actually happened |
| **Expected** | What you hoped/designed for |
| **Mismatch** | The gap between those two things |
| **Likely cause** | Your best guess at why |
| **Decision** | Keep / Remove / Simplify / Clarify / Expand / Test again |

### Step 4 — Name the one thing to fix next

Not a list. **One thing.**

The course is clear: do not change three things at once. You won't know what caused the result. Pick the biggest problem and address that first.

Write it as a hypothesis:

> *"If I [specific change], then [specific expected improvement]."*

Example: *"If I remove the Enchantment's learning capacity benefit and just keep the counter gain, then players will find it easier to understand what Enchantments do."*

## A Note on Critical Feedback

Players are polite by default. Your new player will almost certainly soften their feedback unless you've set up the session correctly.

If their answers to the four questions are all positive, ask this directly:

> *"If you had to give me one piece of feedback that might sting — what would it be?"*

Or: *"Pretend you're a harsh reviewer on BoardGameGeek. What would you write?"*

Reframing like this gives people permission to say what they actually think. The course is explicit: explicitly ask for honest negative feedback, because people default to politeness. That politeness is useless to you.

## Stage Check: What This Session Is For

Based on the game's current state (you are in **Core Loop Testing / Stage 3**), this session is not about:

- Balancing (don't worry about who won or by how much)
- Final polish (ignore any visual roughness)
- Content variety (don't add cards before fixing the core)

This session IS about:

- **Do players understand what they're doing and why?**
- **Does the central mechanic create tension, interest, or delight?**
- **Are the phase transitions (Cast → Recall → Learn) intuitive?**
- **Does the game feel like the thing it claims to be?**

If you can answer yes to all four of those after this session, your next move is to run two or three more sessions to confirm — then consider Gate C passed. If you can't, you have your next problem to fix.

Good luck. Take notes. Don't defend anything.
