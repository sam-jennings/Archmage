# Opus Task: Thematic Coherence — Naming, Narrative, and Terminology
*Created: 2026-05-07 — self-contained brief, no mid-task feedback required*

---

## What you are

You are a senior game designer and editor with expertise in how language shapes player experience. You are working on a card game with a rich thematic premise that is not being felt during play. Your job is to audit every piece of player-facing language — spell type names, turn phase names, mechanical terms, and the Glossary — and produce a coherent revised vocabulary that makes the game's narrative arc visible and its spell types memorable, without changing any mechanics.

You are also fixing a concrete consistency error in the Glossary (described below) that has been left over from an earlier version of the game. All source material you need is included in this brief. Do not ask for clarification — reason through ambiguities yourself.

**Important constraint:** This task touches naming and language only. Do not propose changes to mechanics, scoring, card counts, or turn structure. Do not propose art or graphic design solutions — assume no new art is available. Any solution must work as text printed on existing card surfaces, rulebook pages, and reference materials.

**Coordination note:** A separate task is being run in parallel on the counter refresh rule and turn-stage tracker. That task may produce new phase names as a side-effect. Your phase naming recommendations here should therefore be justified on thematic grounds, not assumed to be final — flag clearly if your phase name choices would conflict with a tracker-first design (where brevity and distinctiveness matter more than elegance).

## The game's intended identity

### Core mechanical experience

Players build a Spellbook — a tableau of learned spells — from four spell types, using cards drawn from a depleting central deck (The Source). The game ends when The Source empties and a final "Released Reserve" is exhausted. Players score based on the size and type of spells in their Spellbook at game end.

### The intended thematic experience (from the project's own description)

> Building patterns of magical components into your Spellbook over the course of the game, racing the depleting Source toward the moment when the magic itself converges on the most powerful wizard. The pleasure is in arranging components into spells, watching your Spellbook grow, and the climactic Drought ending.

> Strategy gamers who enjoy a 30–60 minute tactical card game with thematic integration — the wizard / spell-building fantasy. Specifically players who like building combos and patterns toward an escalating payoff, not abstract optimisers. They want to feel like they're constructing something, not just optimising numbers.

### The visual and tonal direction (from the Visual System Specification)

The game uses **resonance-based ritual magic**: magic understood as an ambient arcane force that can be perceived, crystallized, attuned to, structured, transformed, and internalised. The intended mood is: *dark, arcane, ceremonial, precise, luminous, cosmic, studied, mystical, elegant.*

The ideal feeling: players should feel like they are handling magical diagrams, attuned components, pieces of a formal arcane discipline, visible proofs of mastery.

Not: raw natural elements, fantasy spell illustrations, technological interfaces, random abstract icons.

The best single description of the intended aesthetic: **"Engraved ritual diagrams with living magical motion."**

### The four currents (suits)

The suits are not classical elements. They are four equally fundamental currents of magic:

- **Radiance** — magic that emits (gold/amber)
- **Void** — magic that absorbs (deep violet/indigo)
- **Flux** — magic that moves (teal/turquoise)
- **Aether** — magic that binds and stabilises (crimson/deep red)

These are mechanically equal — no current is stronger than another. They should feel like four modes of the same arcane reality, not four factions with different personalities.

## The story (full text)

This is the lore document — players don't read this during play, but it defines what the mechanics are supposed to feel like.

---

**THE CRISIS**

The Archmage is gone.

Without an Archmage to anchor the flow of magic, the world's arcane currents are collapsing. Ancient wards falter, magical artifacts dim, and the ambient magic flowing through the universe steadily dissipates into the void. What once renewed itself now dwindles with every passing day.

The Grand Academy understands what must happen: when ambient magic reaches critical depletion, it will seek out and elevate the most powerful living wizard, raising them to Archmage. This isn't a title to be awarded or a position to be judged — it's a magical inevitability. The magic itself will choose, converging upon the wizard whose mastery resonates most powerfully.

**THE SUMMONING**

The Academy calls forth its greatest wizards to demonstrate their mastery before the magic makes its choice. What they learn, what they internalise, what they become will determine who the magic elevates.

**UNDERSTANDING & MASTERY**

Throughout their work, wizards demonstrate their understanding through four fundamental patterns:

**Conjuration** proves a wizard can channel pure elemental power — sustaining a direct connection to one fundamental force and drawing additional ambient magic through that resonance.

**Transfiguration** proves a wizard understands transformation — using controlled components as catalysts to attune themselves to Array components. The drained catalysts flow to the Arcane Reserve.

**Enchantment** proves a wizard can achieve internal metamorphosis — permanently altering their own essence by aligning components of identical resonance. These internalised modifications expand a wizard's fundamental capacity, and persist even when all infrastructure collapses.

**Perfect Transmutation** proves absolute mastery within a single element — the ability to both channel raw elemental power AND perform transformation simultaneously, demonstrating complete dominion over one fundamental force.

**THE DROUGHT**

When the Source empties, ambient magic is gone. The Academy's stabilising infrastructure collapses. External spellcasting becomes impossible. Only internal magic (Enchantments) persists. Learning continues — wizards can still refine their Spellbooks with the limited Released Reserve before final evaluation.

**THE ASCENSION**

When the Released Reserve is depleted, the accumulated power in each wizard's Spellbook resonates with the world's concentrated magic. The magic evaluates these resonances and converges upon the strongest. That wizard ascends. The currents stabilise. Magic flows again.

## The current player-facing terminology

### Turn phases (as printed on the Quick Reference)

1. **Collection Phase** — Draw 1 from Source OR take 1 from Array.
2. **Casting Phase** — Cast spells one at a time, resolve effects immediately.
3. **Recall** — Pick up all counters from cast spells and return them to your pool.
4. **Learning Phase** — Use counters for Learn/Empower/Reshape/Unlearn actions.

*(During the Drought, only Collection and Learning occur — no Casting.)*

### Spell types

| Name | Requirement | Effect |
|------|-------------|--------|
| **Conjuration** | 3+ same-energy cards | Draw bonus cards from The Source |
| **Transfiguration** | 3+ sequential-value cards (any energies) | Exchange cards with The Array |
| **Enchantment** | 3–4 matching-value cards | Always active; increases capacity |
| **Perfect Transmutation** | 3+ sequential same-energy cards | Both Conjuration + Transfiguration effects |

### Learning actions

- **Learn** — Place a new spell from hand into Spellbook.
- **Empower** — Add cards to an existing spell.
- **Reshape** — Reorganise components across all spells.
- **Unlearn** — Dissolve a spell, return cards to hand.

### Key game nouns

The Source, The Array, The Arcane Reserve, The Released Reserve, The Drought, The Ascension, Spellbook, Counter, Recall, Recognition Points.

## The Glossary inconsistency (concrete fix required)

The current Glossary contains a terminology error that is a leftover from an earlier version of the game. The following entries still use the old elemental model instead of the current Currents model:

**"Element" entry in Glossary (current, incorrect):**
> Element *(Mechanical)* — One of the four fundamental forces: Fire, Water, Earth, Air. Represented by card suit symbols (♠♥♦♣).

**"Component" entry in Glossary (current, partially incorrect):**
> Component *(Mechanical)* — A card representing a fragment of magical energy. Has an element (Fire, Water, Earth, Air) and a value/resonance frequency (1-20).

The live rulebook uses **Radiance, Void, Flux, Aether** — not Fire, Water, Earth, Air. The four suit names have also been redefined: Spades = Radiance, Hearts = Void, Diamonds = Flux, Clubs = Aether.

Additionally, the Glossary's **"Ambient magic" entry** uses the term "the aether" as a poetic synonym for the magical field:
> Ambient magic *(Story)* — The raw, natural magical energy flowing through the universe. Exists unseen in the aether until drawn into the Source or crystallized in the Array.

But the Visual System Specification explicitly notes that this usage collides with **Aether** as a current name, and recommends renaming the ambient field to **"the ether"** or **"the arcane field"** to avoid confusion.

These three Glossary entries need correcting as part of this task. Provide updated text for each.

## Your task

### Step 1: Diagnose the narrative gap

The story document is evocative and coherent. The mechanics are designed to express it. Yet players don't feel any thematic arc during play.

Diagnose specifically why. Consider:

1. **Language mismatch** — Do the mechanical phase names ("Collection Phase", "Casting Phase", "Learning Phase") carry any narrative weight, or do they describe *what you do* rather than *what it means in the fiction*? Compare them to the language in the story: "gathering ambient magic", "activating patterns", "formalizing understanding."

2. **The arc problem** — The story has a clear three-act structure: building mastery while the Source flows → the Drought, when external magic fails → the Ascension, when magic evaluates what you've built. Does the current naming make these acts feel distinct? Does a player experience "entering the Drought" as a meaningful moment, or just a rule change?

3. **Spell type names** — Do "Conjuration", "Transfiguration", "Enchantment", and "Perfect Transmutation" communicate anything about *why* a player would want to build them? Do they convey the hierarchy — that Perfect Transmutation is a synthesis of all other forms? Do they feel like they belong to the same magical system?

4. **The "just optimising numbers" risk** — The target player wants to feel like they're *constructing something*, not just optimising numbers. What specific language choices are currently pushing the game toward the "optimising numbers" feeling rather than the "building mastery" feeling?

State your diagnosis clearly before proposing any solutions. Identify the two or three changes that would have the most impact.

### Step 2: Audit the spell type names

Evaluate each spell type name against three criteria:

- **Memorability** — Is it easy to say, recall, and distinguish from the others? Would a player who learned the game a week ago remember it under its name?
- **Thematic fit** — Does the name communicate what the spell *means* in the fiction — not just what it *does* mechanically?
- **Hierarchy clarity** — Do the four names together communicate that Perfect Transmutation is the synthesis, Enchantment is the internal/permanent path, and Conjuration/Transfiguration are the foundational types?

Flag any name that fails on any criterion. For "Transfiguration" and "Perfect Transmutation" specifically, the playtest evidence confirms real difficulty — a strategic player said so out loud mid-game. Take this seriously.

### Step 3: Rename or retain each spell type

For each of the four spell types, produce one of:

**(a) A recommendation to retain** — with a specific reason the name is correct and a note on whether it needs any reframing in supporting text.

**(b) A proposed rename** — with at minimum two candidate names, your recommended choice, and the reason. The rename must:
- Fit the resonance-based ritual magic aesthetic (dark, ceremonial, mystical — not elemental-fantasy, not sci-fi)
- Be easy to say and remember
- Distinguish itself clearly from the other three names
- Carry meaning about what the spell *represents*, not just what it *does*

Produce a final table showing all four spell types with their final recommended names.

### Step 4: Audit the turn phase names

Evaluate the four turn stages — Collection, Casting, Recall, Learning — against the same criteria used for spell types, plus one additional criterion:

- **Arc expression** — Does the sequence of phase names, read in order, suggest the narrative rhythm of the game? Do they convey the feeling of "gathering → acting → consolidating → building"?

Then produce revised names if needed. Requirements:
- Each name must be short enough to fit on a tracker or reference card (target: 1–2 words)
- The Drought variant (Collection → Learning, no Casting) should feel like a meaningful contraction of the normal sequence, not an awkward exception
- Recall is a transition step, not a full phase — your naming should make that status clear (either rename it to feel like a transition, or absorb it into an adjacent phase)

**Flag clearly** if any phase name you recommend would be awkward on a physical turn tracker (e.g., too long, too similar to an adjacent phase, hard to abbreviate).

### Step 5: Audit the learning action names

The four learning actions — Learn, Empower, Reshape, Unlearn — are all short and functional. Evaluate whether they carry thematic weight or whether any should be renamed for better fit with the revised vocabulary. A rename here is only warranted if the current name actively conflicts with the thematic identity you're building — do not rename for its own sake.

### Step 6: Fix the Glossary inconsistencies

Using the three incorrect entries identified above ("Element", "Component", "Ambient magic"), produce corrected Glossary text for each. Requirements:
- Replace Fire/Water/Earth/Air with Radiance/Void/Flux/Aether and update the suit symbol mapping (♠ = Radiance, ♥ = Void, ♦ = Flux, ♣ = Aether)
- Resolve the "aether" / "Aether" collision using the Visual System's recommended term ("the ether" or "the arcane field") — choose one and apply it consistently
- Maintain the Glossary's existing format: **Term** *(Type)* — Definition

Also: check whether the renamed spell types or phase names from Steps 3–4 require updates to any other Glossary entries. List any that need changing, with revised text.

### Step 7: Produce the revised vocabulary in full

Compile everything into one reference table showing:
- All four spell type names (old → new, or "retained")
- All four turn phase names (old → new, or "retained")
- All four learning action names (old → new, or "retained")
- The corrected collective term for suits ("Currents" — already established, but confirm it works with the new vocabulary)
- The corrected ambient field term ("the aether" → your choice)

This table is the deliverable — it should be usable directly as a naming spec for updating the rulebook and Glossary.

### Step 8: Integration check

1. Do your revised spell type names and phase names work together as a coherent vocabulary? Read them aloud in the context of explaining the game: "On your turn, you [phase 1], then [phase 2], then [phase 3]. You can learn four types of spells: [A], [B], [C], and [D]." Does that sentence feel like it's describing the same game?
2. Does the revised vocabulary make the three-act arc (flowing Source → Drought → Ascension) feel more visible? Would a player hearing the Drought phase name understand that something fundamental has changed?
3. Are there any collisions or confusions between the new names? (e.g., two names that sound similar, or a spell type name that overlaps with a phase name)
4. Is there any name in your revised vocabulary that would need art or graphic design to carry its meaning — i.e., that only works if a card is illustrated a certain way? If so, flag it, because art is deferred.

### Step 9: Playtest recommendation

Write a one-paragraph brief for how to test whether the naming changes land. Specify: what to observe during the session, what player behaviour would confirm the names are working, and what would indicate the problem is deeper than language.
